Swan

(Cygnus)

     Swans are the largest extant members of the waterfowl family Anatidae and are among the largest flying birds. Swans are almost entirely herbivorous, although they may eat small amounts of aquatic animals.

     Swans mate for life. Swans' nests are on the ground near water and about a metre across. Unlike many other ducks and geese, the male helps with the nest construction, and will also take turns incubating the eggs. The mother spend most time with the cygnets. Dad's main role is to patrol their territory and protect his family.

Eagle

(Haliaeetus leucocephalus)

     Bald eagles can fly 32-64 km miles per hour in normal flight and can dive at speeds of 120-160 km pe hour. They can fly at altitudes of 3000 meters or more.

     Bald eagles have excellent eyesight, large talons and a hooked beak. These features aid eagles in finding, catching and eating their prey. An eagle’s excellent eyesight is used while hunting when soaring high above the ground or from perches overlooking the water.

Swift

(Apus apus)

     Chimney swifts are very fast fliers and spend most of their lives in midair. They eat, drink and mate while flying, only stopping to raise young. They even take their baths in flight by dipping in a water source briefly.

     It’s estimated they fly more than 800 kilometers each day. Common swifts are migratory. Their summer breeding range runs from Portugal and Ireland in the West across to China and Siberia in the East.
