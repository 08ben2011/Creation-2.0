Swan

(Cygnus)

     Swans are the largest extant members of the waterfowl family Anatidae and are among the largest flying birds. Swans are almost entirely herbivorous, although they may eat small amounts of aquatic animals.

     Swans mate for life. Swans' nests are on the ground near water and about a metre across. Unlike many other ducks and geese, the male helps with the nest construction, and will also take turns incubating the eggs. The mother spend most time with the cygnets. Dad's main role is to patrol their territory and protect his family.

Eagle

(Haliaeetus leucocephalus)

     Bald eagles can fly 32-64 km miles per hour in normal flight and can dive at speeds of 120-160 km pe hour. They can fly at altitudes of 3000 meters or more.

     Bald eagles have excellent eyesight, large talons and a hooked beak. These features aid eagles in finding, catching and eating their prey. An eagle’s excellent eyesight is used while hunting when soaring high above the ground or from perches overlooking the water.

Peacock

(Pavo cristatus)

     Peafowl are forest birds that nest on the ground, but roost in trees. They are terrestrial feeders. All species of peafowl are believed to be polygamous.

     The males display in courtship by raising the upper-tail coverts into an arched fan. The wings are held half open and drooped and it periodically vibrates the long feathers, producing a ruffling sound. The cock faces the hen initially and struts and prances around and sometimes turns around to display the tail.

Swift

(Apus apus)

     Chimney swifts are very fast fliers and spend most of their lives in midair. They eat, drink and mate while flying, only stopping to raise young. They even take their baths in flight by dipping in a water source briefly.

     It’s estimated they fly more than 800 kilometers each day. Common swifts are migratory. Their summer breeding range runs from Portugal and Ireland in the West across to China and Siberia in the East.

Badger

(Meles meles)

      Badgers are nocturnal, meaning they are most active at night and sleep during the day. Honey badgers are known for their ferocity.

     They have a resistance to snake venom. While many badgers are social, the American badger is not, and spends most of its time alone.

Crow

(Corvus)

     Members of the genus Corvus can be found on every continent except Antarctica and South America (although other close relatives live there). Like a lot of intelligent animals, most crows are quite social.

     Juvenile birds are frequently seen defending their parents’ nest from predators. The South Pacific crow uses the hooks, which are made from pliable twigs that the crows bend using their beaks and feet into a J-shape, to extract insects from tight crevices.

Fox

(Vulpes vulpes linn)

      It is widely held as a symbol of animal cunning. The red fox has the largest natural distribution of any land mammal except human beings. Their preferred habitats are mixed landscapes, but they live in environments ranging from Arctic tundra to arid desert.

      Fox families, also known as a skulk or leash of foxes, live in underground dens. When the vixen has her pups — a litter of foxes can range between one and 11 pups — they live in the den together for seven months while the pups grow.
